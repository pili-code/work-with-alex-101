# Work with Alex 101

A guide for the whole TDP team on how to work with Alex (Alessandro Alter), cofounder.

This isn't GTM-specific. Whether you're in design, engineering, ops, or sales, the way Alex thinks,
communicates, and makes decisions is consistent. This guide distills it so any update, review, or ask
you bring him lands the first time. It's grounded in a study of real meeting transcripts (see
`analysis/`), but the principles apply to every interaction.

---

## Who Alex is, in one line

Business- and numbers-oriented, fast, hands-on, and allergic to fluff. He wants the conclusion first,
a verified number behind every claim, and a decision to make.

---

## 1. How to communicate with him

- **Lead with the conclusion.** Never walk him through the process to get there. *"Just give us the conclusion so we can talk about what matters."* If he wants the backstory, he'll ask — he rarely does.
- **Be concise and structured.** *"Be more structured and concise; focus on key business results."* Bullets over paragraphs.
- **Small, specific asks beat big ones.** To get feedback fast, send a short, pointed question on **Signal**, not a wall of text. His own words: *"Even if you have an email with three paragraphs, ask 'what do you think about the first paragraph?' Because I do it when I have a few seconds."*
- **Use the right channel:** Signal for quick feedback/urgent, the GTM/ops channel for routine. Don't schedule a call for something a message can answer.

## 2. How to report numbers (this is where trust is won or lost)

- **Every claim gets a number** — a conversion rate, a dollar figure, a count. No number = he stops trusting it.
- **Every number gets a source.** Where did this lead / signup / result come from? "Estimated" is not "tracked," and he will catch the difference.
- **Show trends, not vanity totals.** Up or down vs last week. He wants the delta.
- **Never say "tested" unless you can show it worked.** His single most important rule:
  > *"Don't tell me it's tested and then it's not. Because next time you tell me something, I need to ask you seven questions to trust the answer."*
  One verified number beats five estimated ones. Reliability is your currency with him.
- **Think in unit economics** where relevant: CAC, break-even, margin, revenue quality.

## 3. How to get a decision from him

- **Bring a recommendation, not an open question.** "Here's the situation, here's what I'd do, do you agree?"
- **Frame experiments as test-and-kill.** He's biased to action: *"Why decide up front it'll fail? Test it, we can always kill it."* Give a confidence level and a kill criterion.
- **Separate must-have from nice-to-have.** He protects focus aggressively.

## 4. What frustrates him (avoid these)

- Long narratives that bury the point.
- Claiming something is tracked/tested/done when it isn't verified.
- Vanity metrics with no source, no trend, no action attached.
- Bringing a problem with no proposed next step. Pair every weak metric with "here's what I'll do."

## 5. The pre-send checklist

Before any update, review, or report goes to Alex:
- [ ] Conclusion is at the top
- [ ] Every number has a verified source (not estimated-as-tracked)
- [ ] Every metric has a trend (▲/▼ vs last period)
- [ ] Every finding has a proposed action
- [ ] Decisions are flagged with your recommendation
- [ ] It's short
